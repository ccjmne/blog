I use the Z shell, Zsh. Before using it, I'd acquired some reasonable familiarity with the POSIX shell specification, and the Bourne-Again Shell, Bash. It’s been years since I last pair-programmed extensively with someone who wasn't surprised that many of these agile "tricks" are built right into the plain (albeit Bourne-Again) shell. Yet, certainly not due to more, newer or better tools—you and I do wield (though perhaps not quite use) the same ones after all—my best wizardry is mostly the product of a unique balance of asceticism and aestheticism1.

As a result, I use Zsh, without any shred of Oh My Zsh—though this shouldn't be taken as an indictment: it is a great project, one I've simply "outgrown". I'm not alone in this, but we are few; this article is for those of us that get intimate with their tools (and perhaps even RTFM), and for those of you who look forward to doing just that.

Complete and expand all the things

I found it. The Grail, the one key to bring them all and in the completion system bind them2. I present to you the omni-<Tab>!

zmodload zsh/complist
autoload -U compinit && compinit
autoload -U _generic
zle -C zle-expand-omni expand-or-complete _generic

zstyle ':completion:zle-expand-omni:*' completer _expand_alias _complete _approximate
zstyle ':completion:*' menu select

bindkey '^I' zle-expand-omni
so little, to get you so far already

Use the familiar completion system

cd █
1. begin entering a cd command
cd █
home/ scripts/
2. press <Tab> once for the completion menu
cd scr█
home/ scripts/
3. start typing away, here scr
cd scripts/█
home/ scripts/
4. press <Tab> again to complete

Interact with the menu selection

cd █
1. begin entering a cd command
cd █
home/ scripts/
2. press <Tab> once for the completion menu
cd home/█
home/ scripts/
3. press <Tab> again for the interactive menu
cd scripts/█
home/ scripts/
4. navigate the menu—using other bindings

You'll want to set some bindings to most comfortably navigate the menu in selection mode. You can go up, down, left, right, and even enter a practical dynamic search, all "for free", in your base Zsh shell.

I went with Vim-like bindings, h-j-k-l, while holding the <Meta> button—that's Alt on a keyboard labelled for Windows. I enjoy the same bindings across several 2-dimensional interfaces; such as Vim's quickfix-lists stack3, fzf's search results interface4, tmux's pane resizing, et cet.

I'll be sure to put together some more quick-yet-insightful articles to help you making you feel most at home; until then, man zshzle is a good and plentiful resource.

Expand glob patterns

ls *█
1. begin entering an ls command
ls LICENSE README home/ scripts/ █
2. press <Tab> to expand the matching items

Expand your aliases

alias git='noglob git'
1. set up some alias
git█
2. prepare to use it
noglob git █
3. press <Tab> to expand it

Fairly neat! I figure it's about universal, hence my dubbing it the omni-<Tab>. But there is more.

Some extra niceties for the journey

Note that it is quite intelligent in understanding your intent:

ll█
ls -l --almost-all --human-readable █
expand aliases with your cursor adjacent
ll █
LICENSE README home/ scripts/
complete commands with a cursor disjoined

You may also complete the beginning of an alias to its full name, then expand it, then complete its arguments if you so choose. Unexpanded aliases still get the correct completion options, by the way; how I love this entire system.

You can even "take it back"

If you started typing mv -t new-destination **/*.sh and expanded it, only to find that it put an ungodly amount of entries in your command line, you can still "oops" right out of here without losing your hard-earned pre-expansion command:

bindkey ^_ undo
this is Ctrl-/, I believe I adopted that one from the Zsh-grandmaster Roman Perepelitsa himself

Can't tell that ^_ is Ctrl+/, or that ^[[Z means Shift+Tab? You may find out what escape sequence your key combination triggers in a simple interactive cat!

Just run cat, and type away, quit with Ctrl+C or Ctrl+D. It won't handle every case, such a Tab, Ctrl+D, backspace... But at least it's handy, available, and not too hard to remember: a ubiquitous utility5, no arguments.

That is it: bindkey <mapping> undo. Let's see it in action:

mv -t scripts/ **/*.sh█
preparing to move your scripts to the scripts/ directory

Press <Tab> before executing it, just to be sure...

mv -t scripts/ **/*.sh█
roll it back with the binding of your choice
mv -t scripts/ {utils,bin}/*.sh█
adjust and correct your command
mv -t scripts/ bin/startup.sh bin/check_env.sh bin/restart_services.sh bin/update_configs.sh utils/migrate_files.sh
utils/cleanup_old.sh utils/check_integrity.sh
that's more like it! though I had no idea of these other forsaken piles... future-me can sort it out

This approach is arguably not the most appropriate if your only intention was to "peek" as what the glob would expand to, but hey, when you have it, and it works for "everything", and it can always be undone at the press of a button, why not! I will circle back on that in another article that I hope to put out soon.

Going forward

I am barely scratching the surface of what a neat Zsh set-up can bring, but I believe that, in isolation, these few lines putting together an omni-<Tab> make for a complete unit of information, small and focused enough to be digestible—though maybe stopping to explain what each does would be of some benefit, too.

In the meantime, you might be surprise to see how easy it is to start providing completions for the Z Shell!

Good luck, and have fun!
